*The plain-English explanation of what we found.*

Imagine you are trying to describe a symphony to a friend. You have a recording. Your friend also has a recording. But neither of you has the full recording. You each have a different compressed version, smaller than the original, with different parts emphasized and different parts lost in the compression.

You both listen. You both describe what you hear. How much will you disagree?

The answer turns out to depend on what you are listening to.

If you are listening to a single G chord, the compression does not matter much. A G chord compressed one way sounds like a G chord compressed a different way. Your descriptions will be similar.

If you are listening to the full symphony, with all its complexity, the compressions diverge. One version keeps the violins. The other keeps the brass. Your descriptions will be very different.

Our paper is about the quantum version of this. The "recordings" are quantum states. The "compression" is a non-isometric holographic code, which is how physicists describe what a black hole does to the information inside it. The "two friends" are two observers who each reconstruct the state from their version of the compressed recording.

We asked: how much do the two observers disagree about the entropy of the state? And does it depend on whether the state is simple (like the G chord) or complex (like the symphony)?

The answer is yes, it depends. And we can compute exactly how much it depends.

For simple states, the disagreement shrinks as one over the square root of the observer's size. For complex states, it shrinks as one over the observer's size to the three-halves power. The ratio between these two rates of shrinking is exactly one whole unit of exponent. Not approximately. Exactly.

That integer is the result we are most excited about, and the one we understand least. Integer-valued quantities in physics almost always mean something. They are usually counting something. We do not yet know what this integer is counting.

The paper has the math. This essay has the music.
